Can computers create? The immediate answer depends entirely on how we define "create." Historically, human creativity has been mystified—attributed to divine inspiration, subconscious genius, or sudden neuro-chemical leaps. 

When we evaluate computational systems (such as GANs, Transformers, or diffusion models), we often find ourselves moving the goalposts. If an AI writes a sonnet, we argue it lacks "intent" or "feeling." If it paints, we claim it is merely interpolating a high-dimensional manifold of training data. 

### Defining Creativity: The Margaret Boden Model

Margaret Boden distinguishes three forms of creativity:

1.  **Combinational**: Combining familiar ideas in unfamiliar ways (e.g., poetry, collage).
2.  **Exploratory**: Exploring structured conceptual spaces to generate novel outputs within limits (e.g., writing a jazz solo).
3.  **Transformational**: Mutating the conceptual rules themselves to create something that was previously *impossible* within the original framework (e.g., cubism, non-Euclidean geometry).

### Comparison of Human and Machine Dimensions

| Domain | Human Bottleneck | Machine Bottleneck |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Generative Output** | Slow physical execution | Cheap, infinite generation |
| **Conceptual Mapping** | Subject to fatigue & narrow training | Constrained by training dataset diversity |
| **Quality Filtering** | High intuitive selection | Struggles to judge "artistic merit" autonomously |

Holding machines to the human standard of transformational creativity misses the point. The computer's value lies not in mimicking human intuition, but in uncovering connections in conceptual spaces too large for a human to hold in working memory.
