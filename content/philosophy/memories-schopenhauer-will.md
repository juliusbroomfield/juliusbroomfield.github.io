---
title: "Memories as an Escape of Schopenhauer's Will"
description: "Schopenhauer thought art could temporarily free us from the Will's endless cycle of desire and suffering. I argue that memories might do this even better."
date: 2026-01-05
---

In *The World as Will and Representation*, Arthur Schopenhauer describes the "Will" as an aimless, insatiable force that drives all of existence. Because we are expressions of this Will, we are trapped in a cycle of wanting, briefly satisfying a desire, feeling boredom, and desiring again. For Schopenhauer, life is a pendulum swinging between pain and boredom.

He offered only a few escapes:

*   **Aesthetic Contemplation**: Looking at art, particularly music, where we perceive the "pure Ideas" of the world and forget our individual desires.
*   **Asceticism**: Denying the Will altogether.

However, I want to argue for a third, highly accessible refuge: **Memory**.

### The Epistemic Separation of Memory

When we experience a moment in real-time, the Will is active. We are concerned with outcomes, judgments, and future desires. 

In memory, the moment is static. We look at the past as an observer. The pain of the past is no longer painful in the same way; it is represented. We can contemplate a past sorrow or joy with the same aesthetic detachment that Schopenhauer reserved for viewing a Dutch landscape painting.

> "A memory is a work of art we paint without knowing it, using our own life as the canvas."

Memory removes the immediate pressure of the Will. It allows us to view our own history as pure representation, transforming the random noise of lived experience into a structured, meaningful narrative.
