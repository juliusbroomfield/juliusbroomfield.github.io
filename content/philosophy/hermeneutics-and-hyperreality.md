The modern internet is too vast to be browsed raw. To make it navigable, we rely on feed algorithms, search rankings, and personalized recommenders. In doing so, we delegate our attention to code. 

Jean Baudrillard defined *hyperreality* as a condition in which what is real and what is fiction are seamlessly blended together so that there is no clear distinction for the spectator. Recommender systems do not simply sort information; they curate a hyperreal copy of human interest.

### The Sensorimotor Feedback Loop of the Web

We act on the web, and the web responds. But the feedback loop is increasingly cybernetic:

1.  **Selection**: The algorithm projects a personalized set of options.
2.  **Interaction**: The user clicks, hovers, or scrolls, emitting signals.
3.  **Optimization**: The algorithm refines its state to maximize engagement.

> "The map has preceded the territory." 
> — Jean Baudrillard

In a traditional sense, a map is a representation of a physical space. For the internet, the algorithmic recommendation *is* the territory. There is no "raw" internet behind your feed. The feed is generated dynamically at the moment of request—it exists solely for the observer, and disappears the moment they close the tab.

### Implications for Epistemology

When recommenders construct our reality:
*   **Aesthetic Convergence**: Content creator optimization forces videos, essays, and music to converge toward the medium expected by the model.
*   **Epistemic Fragmentation**: If there is no shared public square, consensus becomes structurally impossible. We reside in custom-tailored simulation bubbles.

As we move toward agentic recommenders and generative search, we will no longer retrieve documents; we will retrieve synthesized explanations designed to satisfy our specific cognitive biases. The project of modern hermeneutics must therefore expand to decode the machine's model of the human observer.
